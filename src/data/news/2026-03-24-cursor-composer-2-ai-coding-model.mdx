Cursor, the AI-powered code editor used by over a million developers daily, just shipped its most significant update yet: **Composer 2**, the company's first in-house AI model built specifically for writing code.

Released on March 19, Composer 2 isn't just another coding assistant that suggests the next line. It's an *agentic* model — meaning it can take a high-level instruction, plan an approach, edit files across your entire project, run terminal commands, and iterate on its own work. Think of it less as autocomplete and more as a junior developer that can handle multi-step tasks.

## What Makes It Different

Previous versions of Cursor relied entirely on third-party models like Claude and GPT. Composer 2 is the first model Cursor has trained itself, giving the company direct control over how it behaves inside its editor.

The results are measurable. On SWE-bench Multilingual — a standard test for AI coding ability — Composer 2 scores 73.7, up from 65.9 on the previous version. It outperforms Claude Opus 4.6 on Cursor's own benchmark, though it still trails GPT-5.4 at higher configurations.

The biggest practical difference is cost: Composer 2 is roughly **86% cheaper** than its predecessor. Standard pricing sits at $0.50 per million input tokens and $2.50 per million output tokens — a fraction of what frontier models charge.

## The Controversy

Three days after launch, [VentureBeat reported](https://venturebeat.com/technology/cursors-composer-2-was-secretly-built-on-a-chinese-ai-model-and-it-exposes-a/) that Composer 2 was built on top of **Moonshot AI's Kimi K2.5**, an open-source model from a Chinese AI lab. Cursor confirmed this on March 22, explaining that it used Kimi K2.5 as a base and applied extended pre-training and reinforcement learning on long-horizon coding tasks.

The disclosure raised questions about transparency in AI model supply chains. Enterprise customers, in particular, want to know what's running under the hood — especially when it involves code that touches proprietary systems. Cursor has since been more explicit about the model's provenance, but the episode highlights an industry-wide gap in how AI companies communicate model origins.

## What This Means for You

If you use [Cursor](/blog/claude-code-vs-cursor/) — or you've been considering it — Composer 2 makes the free and paid tiers significantly more capable. The agentic workflow means you can describe a task like "add authentication to this API" and watch it plan and execute across multiple files, rather than prompting one file at a time.

It's available now on all plans, including Cursor's free Hobby tier. The model runs up to eight parallel agents in [Cursor 2.0's architecture](https://cursor.com/changelog), making it practical for larger refactors and multi-file features.

For developers already using AI coding tools, Composer 2 is worth testing — the performance-to-cost ratio is hard to beat. For everyone else watching the AI space, the real story may be the transparency question: as AI tools become infrastructure, knowing what model powers your tools matters more than ever.
