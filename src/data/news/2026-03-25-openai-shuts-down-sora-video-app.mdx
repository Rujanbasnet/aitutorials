OpenAI is pulling the plug on Sora, its standalone AI video generation app, just six months after it launched to massive hype.

The company announced Tuesday that it is shutting down the app, writing on X: "We're saying goodbye to Sora. To everyone who created with Sora, shared it, and built community around it: thank you." The decision also kills a [$1 billion partnership with Disney](https://www.thestreet.com/technology/openai-is-shutting-down-sora-and-the-disney-deal-is-off) that was announced just three months ago — a deal where no money ever changed hands.

## What Happened

Sora launched in September 2025 and shot straight to the top of Apple's App Store, hitting 1 million downloads in under five days. It was pitched as a TikTok-style social platform where anyone could create AI-generated videos from text prompts.

But the excitement didn't last. Downloads peaked at 3.3 million in November 2025 and fell to about 1.1 million by February 2026. Over its entire life, the app earned just $2.1 million from in-app purchases — a rounding error for a company [seeking a $10 billion raise](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/25/arm-stock-chip-revenue-agi-cpu.html) at a $730 billion valuation.

## Why OpenAI Killed It

OpenAI framed the decision as a resource trade-off. "As we focus and compute demand grows, the Sora research team continues to focus on world simulation research to advance robotics," a spokesperson said. Video generation is vastly more compute-intensive than text, and OpenAI appears to be consolidating around [ChatGPT and its core models](/news/2026-03-14-openai-gpt-5-4-computer-use/) as it prepares for a potential IPO.

But there's another factor: deepfakes. Despite guardrails meant to block generation of public figures, users routinely bypassed them. Deepfake videos of Martin Luther King Jr. and Robin Williams appeared on the platform, prompting both of their daughters to publicly ask people to stop. The [moderation challenges](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/25/g-s1-115055/openai-pulls-the-plug-on-sora-the-viral-ai-video-app-that-sparked-deepfake-concerns) proved harder to manage than OpenAI expected.

## The Disney Fallout

The most dramatic casualty is the collapsed Disney partnership. According to [reports from TheStreet](https://www.thestreet.com/technology/openai-is-shutting-down-sora-and-the-disney-deal-is-off), Disney teams were in a working session with OpenAI on Monday evening. Just 30 minutes later, they learned Sora was being shut down entirely. The billion-dollar deal evaporated overnight.

## What Still Works

The standalone app is gone, but the underlying **Sora 2 video generation model** will remain available inside ChatGPT. If you used Sora primarily through ChatGPT rather than the app, nothing changes for you. Other [AI video tools](/discover/) like Runway, Kling, and Google Veo 2 continue to operate as alternatives.

## What This Means for You

If you were a Sora app user, your content and any remaining credits are effectively gone with the app. Check OpenAI's support page for any final details on account closure.

More broadly, this signals something important about the AI industry in 2026: companies are being forced to pick their battles. Compute is expensive, competition is fierce, and even OpenAI can't do everything at once. The era of launching flashy AI demos and hoping they stick may be ending — what matters now is which products actually justify their compute costs.

For anyone interested in AI video generation, the technology isn't going away. It's just consolidating.
