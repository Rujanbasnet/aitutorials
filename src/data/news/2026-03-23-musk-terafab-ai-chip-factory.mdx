Elon Musk has announced what he calls "the most epic chip building exercise in history" — a $25 billion semiconductor factory called **Terafab**, built jointly by Tesla, SpaceX, and xAI.

The announcement came on March 22 at a theatrical launch event inside Austin's decommissioned Seaholm Power Plant, with Texas Governor Greg Abbott in the audience. The facility will be built on the North Campus of Giga Texas, adjacent to Tesla's existing Austin manufacturing base.

## What Terafab Will Actually Build

The factory targets two chip families. The first is Tesla's fifth-generation AI chip, the **AI5**, designed for self-driving vehicles, the Cybercab robotaxi programme, and [Optimus humanoid robots](/blog/tesla-optimus-robot/). It promises 40–50x more compute performance than its predecessor. The second is the **D3**, a radiation-hardened processor built for SpaceX's orbital satellites and space-based AI workloads.

Tesla is targeting 2-nanometre process technology — the most advanced node currently entering commercial production — with an initial goal of 100,000 wafer starts per month.

## Why Build a Chip Factory?

Musk acknowledged Tesla's existing suppliers — TSMC, Samsung, and Micron — but said their expansion rates are "much less than we would like." He claimed that every chip fab on Earth currently produces only about 2% of the compute he needs across his companies.

The timing is notable: SpaceX [acquired xAI](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-22/elon-musk-says-tesla-xai-spacex-terafab-to-start-in-austin) in an all-stock deal in February 2026, and the three companies now operate under combined strategic leadership.

## The Space Angle

Perhaps the most ambitious claim: Musk said 80% of Terafab's output would go toward **orbital AI satellites**, arguing that solar power in space is roughly 5x more efficient than on Earth's surface, making space-based AI computing cheaper per watt within two to three years.

## Reasons for Scepticism

No firm construction timeline was provided. Analysts point out that a single 2nm fab with 50,000 wafer starts per month typically costs around $28 billion and takes over three years to build in the US. Tesla has zero semiconductor manufacturing experience, and the company's previous in-house compute project — the Dojo supercomputer — was [abandoned in 2025](https://electrek.co/2026/03/22/tesla-spacex-terafab-chip-factory-ai-desperation/) after Musk called it "an evolutionary dead end."

## What This Means for You

For everyday AI users, this won't change anything overnight. But it signals where the AI industry is heading: the companies building AI tools are increasingly trying to control the entire supply chain, from chips to software. If Terafab delivers even a fraction of what Musk promises, it could reshape how AI compute is priced and distributed — eventually making the AI tools you use faster and cheaper.

For now, treat this as a statement of intent rather than a finished product.
